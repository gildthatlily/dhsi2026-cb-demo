{% capture momthoughts2 %}

my mother was a horrible communicator. her wedding advice for auntie cynthia and uncle barry was "the key to a long life together is communication." she wished them luck. i wonder if she was saying that because of her own struggles with communicating her feelings effectively and in non-violent ways. my mother was a sporadically violent woman. she called me a "little piggy who just [liked] to eat and sleep all day." she told me i was underachieving, but made sure to put me in accelerated classes. a few times, she struck me.

*^it's as though i was just supposed to read her mind.*

{% endcapture %}

{% capture momthoughts3 %}

my own mother tried to neutralize signifiers of blackness while trying to make me proud to be black. writing mine a 100 times on paper in third grade -- the graduate student housing apartment we lived in with my great-grandmother because my father was still living in the dominican republic -- because i had a habit of saying "mines." my mother was a stickler for the english language. that was the year we moved from east flatbush to stony brook, my first real encounter with white people en masse. i remember my cousin alyssia telling a story about my mother telling her it was "library," not "liberry."

going back to my mother and her making me write “mine” 100 times for saying “mines,” i remember the hallway of the grad student apartment where we lived — its sterility to be countered by the 100-year-old house we’d live in the following year — where i taped the piece of paper to a door. my mother was placed in a remedial english class at her cuny when she got to the united states. for her accent. my mother grew up in saint vincent, where she started going to school with her older brother a year early and attended girls high school in the capital, where she boarded with a distant cousin who made her do what sounded like an excessive amount of chores and whose child, who my mother said had intellectual disabilities, would pinch my mother. i remember being a teenager and my mother saying these things to my grandmother at sunday dinner in my grandmother’s house in kissimmee, only to have my grandmother say that my mom — as an oldest girl of seven, as a *child* who had been sent away for her betterment — should have told her. my mother went to girls high school in the capital, where she took o-levels and a-levels. she was placed in a remedial english class for the way that she spoke, despite coming from a british colony. when she received her first paper back, her professor asked what she was doing there. my mother said it was where she had been placed. her not wanting me to pick up anything other than standard american english could be read as an internalized anti-blackness. she wanted me to be successful, hardworking, unfailingly brilliant, pristine, and well-behaved. she tightly controlled my movements and told me to never let anyone “slip me a mickey.” she had been over ten years dead when someone finally did. 

the idea that you have to be perfect to do good mostly applies to women of color, who are not allowed to be human, not allowed to make mistakes. 

{% endcapture %}
